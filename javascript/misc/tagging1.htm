I have been interested in the use of UV to check stamps for over 25 years. Canadian stamps have used tagging since the early 60's. Since the early tagging was always observable to the naked eye, I didn't bother with UV. However, I then found out that UV could be used not only for tagging, but also to identify different types of paper and ink. It can lead to interesting new areas of research, even for experts. For example, last night I visited Hans Reiche, an expert in the field of the Canadian Admirals issue. He has studied this issue for over 50 years and has published a number of philatelic books in this and other areas. He was amazed when I showed him the tremendous variety of ink flourescence that could be observed on the 2 cent (red) issues (sheet stamps, coils, war tax, booklets). In the time that I have been following posts on rec.collecting.stamps, I have seen almost no mention of UV use in philately. I, too, would like to see more posts on this topic. In Canada, the specialized catalogues do refer to flourescent paper varieties and numerous articles have been published in The Canadian Stamp News. Various authors have tried to establish methods of identifying and quantifying their findings to relate to others, but I have have found all of them to be incomplete. More paper and ink varieties exist than I have found in any catalog. Collectors in the U.S. (and other countries) also have an abundance of paper (and tagging) varities to examine. In searching through U.S. kiloware, I have come across a number of definitives that have been produced on very bright flourescent paper (whereas the common issue is on very dull paper). A number of years ago, I received mail on 2 consecutive days from the same source in Mexico, with the same stamp issue, but one was dull flourescent and the other was very bright. For those of you who have never experienced the use of UV on stamps, it can open up a whole new world to you. Please let us know your findings. Caution: Paper and ink variety studies should use long wave UV only (an 18 inch flourescent black light works great). Tagging for many countries, such as Canada, can be studied with long wave only. However, short wave UV (much more dangerous to the eyes) is needed to study tagging on some, but not all, issues of countries such as the U.S. and Britain. Have fun, Karl Siemens
