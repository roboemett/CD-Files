Art

Where would we be without art? Probably right here, but we'd be bored and miserable a lot more often.

  • Ukiyo-e, or Japanese woodblock prints, were discovered by the west when they were crumpled up to use as packing for pottery being shipped to France. Now they are a treasured art form.
  • The Corning Museum of Glass is even better in person, but their page is cool too.
  • The Andy Warhol Museum is ... well, you had better just see for yourself!
  • New York's Metropolitan Museum of Art is one of the world's premier art museums.
  • The Norton Museum of Art has nothing to do with Peter Norton (at least, I think it doesn't).
  • The Uffizi Gallery in Florence, Italy is a great place to while away many happy hours. There's great ice cream in Florence, too.

Various Fun Ways to Waste Time

We all do it. Here are some of my favorites.

  • Do you like auctions? Try the AuctionWeb for buying and selling most anything.
  • If you're interested in things nautical, try the Coast Guard's page.
  • Are you really, truly bored? Hop over to URoulette to visit random pages.
  • All the news that's fit to disseminate digitally? Try the New York Times page (and it won't fill up your recycling bin!).
  • The Library of Congress is one of those places that makes paying taxes a bit less unpleasant.
  • Want to buy a book? Amazon.com almost surely has it.
  • Want to torture yourself with beautiful pictures of exotic places you can't visit? Try Conde Nast Traveler.
  • The Wall Street Journal lets you see how that Netscape stock you didn't buy is doing.

Search Engines

I know, I know. Everyone and their aunt has a list of search engines on their page. So why should I be any different?

  • You might call SEARCH.COM a meta-search engine. Rather than providing its own search capabilities, it provides an interface to many different individual search sites. It's easy to get confused here, but when nothing else works it's the place to turn.
  • Magellan is different from many other search sites. Rather than using an automated text or keyword search. Magellan's staff of real people visit each site and review it. Each site receives a one to four star quality rating, a green light symbol if free of "adult" content, and a brief descriptive review. Because of this approach, Magellan is more likely to miss relevant sites than some other search engines, but the "hits" it returns will be real hits and not the 90% moronic output of some of the computerize search methods.
  • Yahoo is perhaps my favorite search engine. It seems to strike a good balance between thoroughness and relevance. Yahoo's appeal is enhanced by its What's New, What's Cool, Random Site, and Headlines links.
  • The All-In-1 site takes a hierarchical subject-based approach to searching. You work your way down from very general subject areas to more specific areas, then are presented with an interface a collection of relevant search engines.
  • The WebCrawler is an automated index generator whose operation is well described by the name. An agent automatically follows links on the web to any and all documents it can find, and the text in those documents is indexed. It is very thorough but dumb as a post. A search for "Java," for example, turned up not only a whole slew of sites on Java programmng but also the URL of some organization that deals with coffee.
  • The Argus/University of Michigan Clearinghouse is best thought of as a guide to Internet guides. Argus lists only Web sites that are themselves guides to Internet sites or other resources dealing with a specific topic. If you want to know where to look for information on aquatic biology, legislation dealing with adoption, or Japanese woodblock prints, then Argus is just the place to look. If you want to find pages dealing with "Doom" or "sex" then you had better look elsewhere.
  • The Open Text Index is another text indexing robot. It's a bit more flexible than most because it lets you specify where in the target document the text is found (URL, title, heading, summary, etc.) and to combine individual searches with logical operators. For example, you could locate document that have "JavaScript" in the title and "verification" in the summary.
