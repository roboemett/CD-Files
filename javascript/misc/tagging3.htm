Robin is in public relations and is not a technical expert but I would like to thank him publically for trying: 1) First, we do have a specification for brightness of stamp paper. The USPS specifications for stamp, as indicated in both our specification for gum stamp paper (USPS-P-1191) and our specifications for the regular self-adhesive stamps (USPS-P-1238), and for linerless stamps (USPS-S-1282) all specify a paper brightness (for the print side) of 81 minimum when measured in accordance with TAPPI (The Technical Association of the Pulp and Paper Industry) T452. The reason this minimum brightness is specified is that it directly relates to print quality, specially when very high line screen printing (minimum 300 lines per inch or more) is required for most commemorative stamps printed by gravure or offset (lithography). 2) The second question concerns the appearance of the stamp taggant under short and long wave UV light. The stamp phosphor taggant is activated only by short wave UV light at 254 NM (nanometers), when it glows as bright green with an emission of light centered at 525 NM. This bright green color may appear dull green, or some other variation of green depending on the background, the carrier varnish in the case of post tagging, or other coatings on the paper in the case of prephosphor paper stamp paper. At any rate, postal equipment that detects the phosphor signal such as the Model 2C phosphor meter or the advanced facer canceler system (AFCS) have the appropriate band pass filters to reject extraneous signals and measure only the light emitted at 525 NM. The Postal Specifications for stamp taggant require that the signal for red fluorescent signal at 615 NM shall not exceed 3 Postal Phosphor Meter Units (PMUs). The long wave UV light usually detects fluorescent brighteners which are present in most commercially available paper but usually not in stamp paper.
